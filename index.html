Earth shaking events around the globe


Earthquakes are a result of tectonic plate shifting under the surface of the earth. These plates, when moved, drag the part of crust around and if two plate scrape one another they produce powerful vibrations that can cause mass destruction. These deadly vibrations are not only limited to the nature's wrath of tectonic shifting only, sometimes even rocks some man-made events can also cause major destruction. However, these events are quite tame when compared to EQ events.

This visualization aims to show the intensity of the EQ events against some other earth-shaking events, both natural and man-made. We first take a look at all the events detected globally corresponding to the earth-shaking categories as follows,



EarthQuakes around the globe


We have the events plotted over a globe according to the provided latitude and longitude values. In this visualization, the world-110m map is used as the basemap, and the events are laid out over it. The visualization can be rotated to view the events at different positions on the globe.

On one hand, we have the events plotted where the event type is anything but earthquake and on the second depiction, we plot the event types that are of earthquake type. It is quite visible from the comparison that majority of the events recorded are of earthquake. Due to lesser number of events, the marker size for `other` event types has been increased.




Magnitude comparison


Now that we know the frequency and trend of occurrences of the events in the data set, we can look at the extent of magnitudes of different event types, their mean value, and the outliers if any that exist for that specific category. From the box plots, we can see that the most outliers are in the earthquake category, no surprises here. We also observe that almost all the events have a mean magnitude < 2, except for both mining categories.

This visualization shows the power of the earthquake type events. Even though other natural events do exist that can cause destruction, but non comes close to the magnitude outliers of the earthquake type events.





Average event magnitudes


Finally, now we can try to visualize the average magnitude for the events. With the averaging included, the outliers now do not pose an issue for plotting axes.

The focus is on monthly comparison of average magnitude over the year between 2019 and 2020. In accordance with the boxplot, we see that the earthquake category has a mean value around 2. This tells us that even though the earthquake events have outlier magnitude values, but they're so frequent in lower magnitude that those values take over the mean of the ensemble.

This visualization provides an interactive legend which can be used to separate out the event type and their average monthly magnitude when selected.
